Prioritization Frameworks That Don't Suck

January 29, 2025

Every product team has a backlog that is too long and too many stakeholders who want their thing first. Prioritization frameworks promise to make the decision objective. In reality they often make it slower and more political. The framework becomes a weapon. Here is how to use structure without the theater.

Why Frameworks Fail

They assume you have good options. If your backlog is a mix of real bets and legacy "we should do this someday" ideas, scoring them just gives you a ranked list of noise. Clean the backlog first. Kill or park the things that are not real candidates for the next cycle. Then prioritize what is left.

They hide the real criteria. When you run RICE or value vs effort, someone still has to assign the numbers. Those numbers are opinions. The framework gives them a veneer of objectivity. Better to make the criteria explicit: "We are optimizing for X this quarter. Does this move X? By how much?" Then argue about that, not about a score.

They delay the decision. A two-hour prioritization meeting where everyone scores 20 items is often a way to avoid saying no. The real decision is: what are we not doing? Say it out loud. Then use a framework only to order the few things you are doing.

What Works Better

One primary criterion per horizon. For the next quarter, pick one thing you are optimizing for: revenue, retention, a specific outcome for a segment, or de-risking a bet. Then ask of every candidate: does it move that? If not, it does not get in. You do not need a matrix. You need a filter.

Fewer things in the room. Do not prioritize 50 items. Have a short list of real options (five to ten) that are already scoped and deemed worth considering. Prioritize that. If something is not on the list, it is a no for now. Document the nos so they do not keep coming back.

A single decider. The best prioritization is not a committee. It is a clear decider (product lead, founder, whoever owns the outcome) who listens to input and then decides. The framework can inform the decider. It should not replace them.

When to Use a Framework

Use a framework when you need to compare like with like: several similar initiatives, similar cost, and you need an order. Use it to force explicit criteria (reach, impact, confidence, effort, or whatever you choose). Do not use it to avoid the judgment call. The judgment call is the job.

The Bottom Line

Prioritization is not about scoring everything. It is about choosing a focus, saying no to most things, and ordering the rest. Use a simple filter first. Use a framework only on the short list. And make one person responsible for the call.
